Self-Retraction as Redemption: Encouraging Repentant Authors to Correct the Scientific Record

This section introduces the Hierarchical Self-Retraction Forgiveness (HSRF) model, which provides a structured framework to incentivize voluntary correction of the scientific literature while mitigating reputational and systemic risks.

Read Abstract View Resources

Abstract

While the scientific community traditionally responds to misconduct through penalties, the potential for encouraging self-retraction by waiving such punishments is less explored. The HSRF model addresses this by providing a hierarchical framework to enable authors and their supporting institutions to retract publications voluntarily without fear of social or institutional penalties.

Robust safeguards prevent misuse of the mechanism. The model emphasizes transparency, honesty, and cooperation while granting forgiveness to repentant authors and the intermediate layers of the scientific system that assist in retraction. By fostering self-correction and reducing systemic barriers, HSRF aims to enhance the integrity of the scientific record.

For full details, the original publication is available at Accountability in Research, 2026.

Principles of HSRF

Transparency & Honesty

All actions and disclosures must be truthful and fully transparent; violations revoke forgiveness.

Retraction as Precondition

Forgiveness is granted only for publications that are formally retracted, not revised.

Covers Fraud, Not Mistakes

HSRF addresses deliberate misconduct; honest errors are handled separately and do not require forgiveness.

Integrity of Process

Any breach of HSRF conditions by authors or institutions may cancel previously granted forgiveness.

Exclusion of Paper Mills

Entities engaged in systematic fraudulent publication production are ineligible for HSRF.

Open Publication of Records

All retractions and HSRF records are publicly accessible to ensure transparency and accountability.

HSRF Processes

1. Granting SRF in Proactive Disclosure

The author or layer voluntarily submits a detailed self-retraction request. The Forgiveness Authority evaluates, investigates if necessary, makes a decision, announces publicly, and archives the record.

2. Deciding SRF in Reactive Response

When a third party reports potential misconduct, the Authority evaluates the claim, engages relevant layers, reviews their responses, and decides whether to grant forgiveness.

3. Cancellation of Granted SRF

If subsequent evidence shows violations of HSRF principles, the Authority can revoke previously granted forgiveness, notify affected layers, and update the records accordingly.

The HSRF model ensures both efficiency and integrity, balancing encouragement of self-retraction with safeguards against abuse.
