# My Experience ## Ignored Warnings: A Case Study of Scientific Integrity, Institutional Response, and Whistleblowing ### Overview This report provides the background to Lesson One, an independent long-term initiative exploring scientific misconduct, scientific correction, research integrity, and the institutional dynamics of scientific self-regulation. The events described here began during my doctoral studies and later contributed to the development of broader research questions concerning how scientific systems respond to allegations of misconduct, how correction mechanisms operate in practice, and why credible warnings may sometimes fail to produce meaningful institutional action. The complete documentation, evidence, figures, references, and supporting materials are available in the accompanying PDF report. --- ## Background In 2017, while completing my doctoral studies, I became concerned about the integrity of several research projects conducted within my academic environment. Based on my observations, participation in defense sessions, and consultations with experienced specialists, I concluded that some reported experiments and measurements required further scrutiny. Before publication of the relevant studies, I communicated my concerns privately and professionally to the supervisory team involved. My expectation was that the concerns would be examined and, if necessary, addressed before publication. However, the publications later appeared substantially unchanged. This experience initiated a long-term effort to document the concerns, communicate with journals and institutions, and better understand the processes through which scientific systems investigate and respond to allegations of misconduct. --- ## Case One: The Lost Field The first case concerned a field experiment reported in agricultural publications. The central question was whether the reported experimental location and conditions corresponded to an experiment that had actually been conducted as described. During my review of the publications and related dissertation materials, I observed substantial similarities between reports that described different locations and environmental conditions. I also raised questions regarding the reported experimental site and presented supporting observations during communications with supervisors and later with journals and institutional bodies. Despite these concerns, the publications remained unchanged. The case ultimately became important not only because of the specific allegations involved but also because it raised broader questions regarding verification, documentation, and institutional evaluation of research claims. --- ## Case Two: Phantom Equipment The second case involved concerns regarding equipment, measurement methods, environmental data, and reported experimental procedures appearing across multiple publications. Several instruments and measurement systems were reported in published studies. Based on my investigations, I questioned whether some of these instruments were available or used in the manner described. Additional concerns emerged regarding: * reported measurement methodologies; * consistency between dissertations and published papers; * environmental monitoring data; * and similarities among figures and datasets appearing in multiple outputs derived from the same research program. These concerns were communicated to journals, publishers, authors, and institutional ethics bodies. Although some investigations were conducted, the publications remained in the scientific literature. --- ## Interactions with Journals and Institutions Beginning in 2020, I contacted multiple stakeholders, including: * scientific journals; * academic publishers; * institutional ethics committees; * governmental and regulatory bodies; * and organizations responsible for oversight of scientific conduct. Over several years, I submitted documentation, correspondence, and requests for clarification. Responses varied considerably. Some organizations acknowledged receipt of the concerns and initiated reviews. Others declined to take action or concluded their investigations without determining that corrective measures were necessary. From my perspective, these experiences revealed important challenges in the practical implementation of scientific correction systems. --- ## The Emergence of Lesson One As the process continued, my focus gradually shifted. Initially, my objective was limited to addressing specific cases that I believed required correction. Over time, however, a broader question emerged: Why do scientific systems sometimes struggle to respond effectively to credible concerns, even when substantial evidence is presented? This question ultimately became the foundation of Lesson One. Lesson One is therefore not merely a record of particular allegations or disputes. Rather, it is an effort to understand the larger institutional, cultural, and procedural factors that influence scientific self-correction. --- ## Challenges of Whistleblowing The experience also highlighted the personal and professional difficulties that may accompany the reporting of suspected misconduct. Over the years, the process involved legal disputes, administrative challenges, unsuccessful appeals, and various professional obstacles. While the causes of every subsequent event cannot be established with certainty, the experience illustrated the complex environment in which whistleblowers may operate. For this reason, Lesson One examines not only misconduct itself but also the institutional conditions surrounding accountability, transparency, correction, and protection of those who raise concerns. --- ## Lessons Learned Several lessons emerged from this experience: * Evidence alone does not always lead to correction. * Scientific self-correction can be significantly slower and more complex than commonly assumed. * Institutional incentives may influence how concerns are evaluated. * Transparency and accountability remain essential components of scientific trust. * Long-term persistence is often required when pursuing unresolved integrity concerns. These observations motivated subsequent research projects within Lesson One, including work on scientific correction systems, governance, and the Hierarchical Self-Retraction Forgiveness (HSRF) model. --- ## Documents and Resources Readers interested in the complete documentation may consult: * Full English report (PDF) * Lesson One (Farsi) (PDF) * Related publications * Public discussions and updates * Future research outputs associated with Lesson One --- ## Closing Reflection This project began with a specific concern about scientific integrity. Over time, it evolved into a broader inquiry into how scientific institutions identify problems, evaluate evidence, and maintain public trust. Regardless of the outcome of any individual case, the central question remains the same: How can scientific systems become more capable of recognizing mistakes, correcting them, and learning from them? That question continues to guide Lesson One.
